Supporting Missionaries with Eating Disorders
Tell Me More

"Most of the sister missionaries and many of the elders in my mission experienced either a full blown eating disorder or extreme disordered eating.”

Missions can be a wonderful blessing to both the missionaries and those they teach. However, missions separate those serving from their family and friends, leaving them in need of the Mission Presidency’s support. Mission Leadership can provide watchful care, informed council, and create inspired rules and guidelines for the missionaries under their protection.




Do Eating Disorders Affect Missionaries?

Missionaries are particularly susceptible to eating disorders

How many missionaries could be struggling?

Among people ages 18 to 29, an average of 1 out of every 47 have experienced an eating disorder in their lifetime (Udo & Grilo, 2018).

Who is most at risk?

Women are more likely to struggle with eating disorders than men (Lipson et al., 2016). In their lifetime, women are 12 times more likely to have anorexia nervosa, 5.8 times more likely to have bulimia nervosa, and 3.01 times more likely to have binge eating disorder (Udo & Grilo, 2018).

When do eating disorders start?

On average, the age that people start having eating disorders is 19.3 for anorexia nervosa, 20 for bulimia nervosa, and 24.5 for binge eating disorder (Udo & Grilo, 2018).

Personal Stories

  • A Mission Wide Problem

    Most of the sister missionaries and many of the elders in my mission experienced either a full blown eating disorder or extreme disordered eating. Many of the sisters had unresolved anorexia before the mission, and the stressors of missionary life exasperated these problems to an extreme. The ideas of perfectionism and that we should forget ourselves and go to work are important, but it can cause missionaries to stop taking care of themselves physically and mentally to the point where they might have to get sent home because of the eating disorders.

  • Taking Care of Companions

    On the mission, I had friends who wouldn’t eat and would make comments about their bodies. It was up to me to remind them to eat and take care of themselves, but sometimes it was out of control. I saw the implications of eating disorders on my mission and how they affected others.

  • An Unaddressed Issue

    Food always seemed like a sensitive topic on my mission. I had two companions in particular that would make a lot of comments about weight. I could tell that one of them was making it a silent competition between me and her to see who could eat less. She was also very passionate about exercise. Because of her comments, I knew she was worried about gaining weight on the mission. We never talked about it, but I think she might have had an eating disorder.



How to Help

Mission leaders are in a better position than parents or friends back home because they are physically present with the missionaries and have greater influence and more opportunities to support them

Education & Awareness

  1. Communicate with parents about not commenting on their missionary’s weight
  2. Use zone conferences to teach missionaries about eating disorders so they can identify it in themselves and in companions
    • Increase Sister Training Leaders’ awareness of signs of eating disorders
    • Teach that young adults’ bodies are still growing. It is developmentally normal to gain weight during these ages

Health and Wellness Initiatives

  1. Find medical/therapy assistance that involves use of religion (Spangler, 2010 & Emmett, 2007)
  2. Help missionaries focus on what their bodies can do, instead of what their bodies look like, by creating a positive culture around exercise
    • It is possible that some missionaries would enjoy mission goals about increasing their health through strength training
    • You could have missionaries create personal goals related to physical strength and have rewards given out to missionaries privately
    • Missionaries could benefit from gaining access to apps that help them do strength training during daily exercise

Policy & Structural Support

  1. Do not encourage missionaries to work so "hard" that they skip dinner
  2. If appropriate, schedule a specific time that the entire mission to eats dinner
  3. Do not encourage fasting more than once a month
  4. Providing ample money for missionaries to afford food
  5. Account for money that it takes to travel when missionaries are in leadership positions
  6. Screen missionaries who may be at risk using a body assessment to figure out who may need clinical assistance (Spangler, 2010), (Kass, A. E. et al., 2017)

What are eating disorders?

Eating disorders are severe and persistent eating behaviors connected to distressing thoughts and emotions. There are many types of eating disorders, but the main disorders that we will focus on are anorexia nervosa (AN), bulimia nervosa (BN), and binge eating disorder (BED).

People with AN starve themselves. Those with BN switch between dieting and then binge eating high calorie foods. Individuals with BED eat large amounts of food in a short period of time and then feel like they lose control over what and how much they are eating.

How is a missionary personally affected?

Eating disorders do not only hinder a missionary's ability to find fulfillment on the mission, they also can hurt a missionary’s mental and physical health. For example, anorexia nervosa not only is associated with depressive disorder, it also predicts it. Research has also shown that suicide is a common cause of death among those with an eating disorders (Berkman et al., 2007).

By analyzing and combining several studies on eating disorders, researchers found that some people who had anorexia nervosa, bulimia, or binge eating disorder experienced a range of negative outcomes, depending on their disorder. These byproducts included excess weight loss, weight regain, organ damage, mood disorders, major depression, anxiety, OCD, social phobia, substance abuse, and death (Berkman et al., 2007).

How is a missionary's service affected?

On average, about half of the people who have struggled with an eating disorder in the last twelve months report that their disordered eating interferes with normal daily activities, results in serious problems with getting along with others, and contributes to serious problems with fulfilling responsibilities (Udo & Grilo, 2018).

Eating disorders can also greatly impact one's ability to be productive and effective (Filipova et al., 2016). Because of these challenges, missions can be particularly difficult for young adults experiencing eating disorders. A mission is full of tight schedules, trying to work with new people, and learning new responsibilities.

How can I identify an eating disorder?

A pilot program performed on two university campuses found that more than half of the individuals who were overweight/obese were also high risk for an eating disorder. They explained that individuals who were overweight or obese were more likely to support binge eating and fasting, report that their eating disorder concerns hindered their social life and made them feel badly, and describe more weight/shape concerns (Kass, A.E. et al., 2017).

However, it is important to remember that Weight is not always an accurate predictor. In a systematic review of literature, they found that about half of those with anorexia nervosa had a normal body weight (Berkman et al., 2007).

Personal motives behind eating disorders

Need for Control

Young women have been found to practice disordered eating because they feel a lack of control. This could be as a reaction to the stress in their lives. When they feel like they can’t control scary things happening in their lives, they find comfort in controlling what they eat (Budd, 2007).

Missionary life includes major eating changes for many missionaries because of a few factors, including the prevalence of meals fed by members of the church in local areas, where portion sizes are out of their control.

Missionaries are also limited in the exercise they can do. Exercise can be done excessively, which is part of these disorders, but can also improve body satisfaction.

Body Image

Eating disorders are also connected to body image. Having negative perceptions of one's body may lead to eating disorders (Ferreiro et al., 2011). It isn't the way that one actually looks, but the person's perceptions of their body size that predicts mental health problems (Al Mamun et al. 2007).

Christian women can judge their bodies harshly. In a study done on eating disorders in Christian women, researchers found that Christian women were rated higher for body checking, which can lead to binge eating (Wilhelm, L., et al., 2018). Sister missionaries are immersed in a Christian culture and are encouraged to present themselves in a way that represents The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-Day Saints. It is possible that their expectations and beleifs affect the way they feel about their bodies.

Perfectionism

Research has found that perfectionistic qualities are associated with eating disorders (Shafran et al., 2002). Many young women who choose to go on missions are high-achieving and come from families with high expectations. This can make them more likely to have perfectionistic tendencies.

Environmental motives behind eating disorders

Lack of Social Support

Young women have been found to practice disordered eating because they feel a lack of control and they need social support (Budd, 2007). Young women report that separating themselves from a dysfunctional source of social support or developing a healthy and close relationship helped them in their recovery from an eating disorder. Their stories emphasized the importance of distancing themselves from people who commented negatively about people's bodies or encouraged unhealthy eating behaviors.

Cultural Pressure

There is a lot of cultural pressure on missionaries to not gain weight. Missionaries are also told that they will unavoidably gain weight, which can be stressful. Members can pressure missionaries to eat more, and companions or family can make unhelpful comments on weight, creating a stressful environment.

Hiding Behind Religious Practices

Missionaries can also miss out on social support by hiding behind religious practices. For example, missionaries can easily skip meals under the disguise of "working hard." There can also be mission or companion expectations to skip meals for the sake of more efficient missionary work. Missionaries can also hide their disordered eating under the disguise of religious fasting. Missionaries can also hide their disordered eating under the disguise of religious fasting.

Stressful Environment

The mission is a stressful time and could cause flare ups in disordered eating. Research has shown that those with bulimia nervosa experience a rise in sadness, guilt, and fear before they binge or purge (Berg et al., 2012). It is possible that sister missionaries use binging and purging as a way to cope with sadness, guilt and fear in their lives.

Because adjusting to missionary life requires getting used to new cultures, creating relationships with companions and other members, and focusing on becoming a proper and hard-working missionary, missionaries can be in a particularly stressful environment that contributes to engaging in poor coping mechanisms like practicing bulimia nervosa.
